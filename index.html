KALPANA CHAWLA
Indian-American Astronaut



An Inspirational Story for Many Aspirants

Who's Kalpana Chawla?

Born in Karnal, India, Kalpana Chawla tragically passed away on February 1, 2003, above the southern United States during the Space Shuttle Columbia's entry, just 16 minutes before its scheduled landing. Kalpana Chawla had a passion for flying, hiking, backpacking, and reading. She held various licenses including a Certificated Flight Instructor's license with airplane and glider ratings, Commercial Pilot's licenses for single and multi-engine land and seaplanes, as well as Gliders, and instrument rating for airplanes.

Her Educational Journey

Completed schooling at Tagore School in Karnal, India, in 1976. Achieved a Bachelor of Science degree in aeronautical engineering from Punjab Engineering College, India, in 1982. Earned a Master of Science degree in aerospace engineering from the University of Texas in 1984. Obtained a Doctorate of Philosophy in aerospace engineering from the University of Colorado in 1988.

First Space Flight Mission

STS-87Columbia mission took place from November 19 to December 5, 1997. It marked the fourth U.S Microgravity Payload flight, focusing on experiments aimed at understanding the effects of weightlessness on various physical processes and observing the Sun's outer atmospheric layers. During the mission, two crew members conducted an Extra-Vehicular Activity (EVA) or spacewalk, which included manually capturing a Spartan satellite and testing tools and procedures for future Space Station assembly. STS-87 completed 252 orbits of the Earth, covering a distance of 6.5 million miles in 376 hours and 34 minutes.

Second Space Flight Misson

STS-107 Columbia mission occurred from January 16 to February 1, 2003. Spanning 16 days, this flight was exclusively dedicated to scientific research. Operating in two shifts around the clock, the crew effectively carried out about 80 experiments. Unfortunately, the STS-107 mission came to an unexpected halt on February 1, 2003, as Space Shuttle Columbia and its crew tragically perished during re-entry, just 16 minutes before the scheduled landing.
